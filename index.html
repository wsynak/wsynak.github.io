GLOBAL REMITTANCES

Remittances, the money sent from migrant workers to their home countries, are the

second highest

(after foreign direct investments) source of external financing in developing countries amounting to

$466 billion in 2017

. In many countries they account for as much as

30% of GDP

and are larger than all other capital flows together. While remittances increase households’ disposable income, it is also argued that they may contribute to higher wealth inequality since it’s usually the rich, who migrate. . The visualization conveys important, from the point of view of economists, policy makers and migrants, information about this category of capital flows.

Remittance flows over time

Line chart below represents trends in remittance flows across countries from 2007 to 2017. You can look at the remittances send or recieved, measured in current US dollars or as a share of country's GDP.

What countries send and recieve the most?

Below you can see countries that recieve the most - India, Philipinnes , Mexico. Biggest sender countries include: United States, Saudi Arabia and Switzerland. Colors of the circle correspond to remittances as a share of Gross Domestic Product. Their sizes represents total values, showed also on the bar chart. You can choose between inflows and outflows, as well as the year.

Billateral Flows

This chart displays World Bank estimates for remittance transfers between countries in 2017. Size and color of the country marks correspond to total remittances inflows or outflows (according to selected category). When you select and click on a country mark, the bar chart will display the top 10 economies in terms of remittances sent / recieved. If no data point is selected, the bar chart displays the highest receiving or sending country.
